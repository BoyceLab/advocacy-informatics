# CMSS/PLRC Patient-Led Research Scorecards

## Stack Levels Most Relevant
> **Organization (8), Role (7), Goals/Functions (6), Workflow/Adoption (5)**

---

## Overview

The **Patient-Led Research Scorecards** were developed through a collaboration between the **Council of Medical Specialty Societies (CMSS)** and the **Patient-Led Research Collaborative (PLRC)**, funded through a PCORI Eugene Washington Engagement Award.

The scorecards address a core problem in patient engagement: **"engagement" is often performative rather than substantive.** A patient on an advisory board who has no power to change study design is not a research partner — they are a prop.

The scorecards provide a structured, validated rubric to evaluate how effective a patient group and research partner collaboration will be at conducting **truly patient-led research**.

---

## The Four Scorecard Domains

### 1. Patient/Partner Governance

> *Evaluates the degree to which decision-making power and governance is shared between patient groups and partner groups.*

This domain addresses the **above-the-line** question of organizational structure: who has authority? Governance includes decisions about funding, study design, publication rights, and data ownership.

**Key indicators:**

- Is decision-making for significant decisions (funding, design, publication) well communicated and agreed upon?
- Is there a written agreement — not just an informal understanding — of rules of engagement?
- Are defined consequences in place if the research organization does not follow through?
- Is deference given to the patient group with sufficient support to exercise that deference meaningfully?

### 2. Integration into the Research Process

> *Evaluates the degree to which patients are involved in every phase of the research process and key committees, including study design, protocols, trial inclusion, analysis, and reporting.*

This domain evaluates whether patients are involved **throughout** the research lifecycle — not just at the beginning (setting priorities) or end (disseminating findings), but at every stage.

**Key indicators:**

- Are patients involved in study design and protocol development?
- Do patients serve on data safety monitoring boards and analysis committees?
- Do patients review and approve manuscripts before submission?
- Do patients contribute to dissemination strategy?

### 3. Patient Burden

> *Evaluates the degree to which patient burden and associated trauma is addressed, including accommodating patients who are dealing with illness and symptoms, compensation for patients' time and skills.*

This domain is often underdiscussed in academic settings. Patient researchers are often people who are also managing serious illness — fatigue, cognitive symptoms, pain, treatment side effects. Research participation adds to that burden.

**Key sub-domains:**

=== "Accessible Engagement"
    Can patients with the disease actually participate? Are meeting times, formats, and locations accessible? Is remote participation genuinely supported? Are materials provided in accessible formats?

=== "Trauma-Informed Practices"
    Does the research organization recognize that discussing one's disease can be traumatic? Are trauma-informed practices in place? Are resources and support provided proactively — not just upon request?

=== "Compensation"
    Are patients compensated at or above market rate for their expertise and experience? Are expenses covered? Are multiple payment options offered? Is compensation set *by patients*, not *for patients*?

=== "Responsiveness to Patients"
    Are there clear, accessible, safe channels for patient input? Does feedback result in actual changes — not just acknowledgment?

### 4. Research Organization Readiness

> *Evaluates the ability of the research organization to engage in meaningful patient partnership.*

This domain acknowledges that many research organizations lack the infrastructure, training, and cultural readiness for meaningful patient partnership — even when they intend to do it well.

**Key indicators:**

- Does the organization have dedicated infrastructure for patient collaboration?
- Is there at least one dedicated staff person focused on meeting the patient group's needs?
- Does the organization conduct routine training on meaningful patient engagement?
- Is the organization aware of and actively working on its own biases?
- Can other patient groups attest to a positive working relationship?

## The Five-Point Scale

Each domain is rated on a five-point scale from **Non-collaboration** (−2) to **Ideal collaboration** (+2):

| Score | Level | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **+2** | Ideal Collaboration | Patients co-lead; systemic processes for input; deference given with support |
| **+1** | Great Collaboration | Strong infrastructure; deference to patients; proactive practices |
| **0** | Acceptable Collaboration | Basic structures in place; patient input acknowledged and sometimes incorporated |
| **−1** | Minimal Collaboration | Some engagement, but ad-hoc and limited; patients have minimal power |
| **−2** | Non-Collaboration | No meaningful patient power; possible harm to patients through participation |

## Applying the Scorecards: A Case Study Exercise

!!! example "Exercise: Score This Collaboration"

    A pharmaceutical company is partnering with an ALS PAG to conduct a natural history study. Here is what they've described:

    - The PAG nominates two patients for an advisory board that meets quarterly
    - The advisory board can review but not modify the study protocol
    - Patients are not compensated ("it's an honor to serve")
    - The research team prepares all publications; the PAG receives a copy before submission but has 48 hours to comment
    - Meetings are held in-person in Chicago with no remote option

    **Task:** Score this collaboration on each of the four domains using the −2 to +2 scale. Justify your scores using specific scorecard criteria. What would need to change to reach "Great Collaboration"?

## Informatics Stack Connection

The Scorecard framework maps directly onto the Stack's **Organization and Role** levels:

| Scorecard Domain | Informatics Stack Level |
|---|---|
| Governance | Organization (8) — who has authority |
| Research Process Integration | Role (7) — are patients in every role? |
| Patient Burden | Workflow/Adoption (5) — is this sustainable? |
| Research Org Readiness | Organization (8) — does the org have capacity? |

A well-designed research informatics system — a registry, a FHIR-enabled data commons, a PRO collection app — supports patient engagement at every Stack level. But **technology cannot substitute for governance**. A beautiful registry platform with no patient decision-making authority is still a −2 on the Governance domain.
